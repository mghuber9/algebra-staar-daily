Day 55 – Rate / Distance Systems

Goal: Practice skills with Rate / Distance Systems.

← Back to all lessons
Key Idea (Rate / Distance Systems):
Use the formula d = rt (distance = rate × time). For two moving objects, set up one equation for each, then use the fact that distances or times are related.

Example (General Algebra)

Two cyclists start at the same point and ride in the same direction. One rides 10 mph and the other rides 14 mph. After 3 hours, how far apart are they?

Step 1 – Distances:

  • Slower cyclist: d₁ = 10(3) = 30 miles
  • Faster cyclist: d₂ = 14(3) = 42 miles

Step 2 – Distance apart:

  • 42 − 30 = 12 miles apart

Here we didn’t need a full system, but d = rt was the key idea.

Your Turn (General Algebra)

A car travels 60 miles in 1.5 hours at a constant speed. What is its speed?

Show answer

d = rt → 60 = r(1.5).
r = 60 ÷ 1.5 = 40 mph.

STAAR-Style Practice

1. A bus and a car leave the same city, driving in opposite directions. The bus travels 50 mph and the car travels 60 mph. How far apart are they after 2 hours?

  1. 50 miles
  2. 60 miles
  3. 110 miles
  4. 220 miles

2. Two trains leave the same station, traveling in the same direction. Train A travels 70 mph and Train B travels 55 mph. How long will it take Train A to be 30 miles ahead of Train B?

  1. 1 hour
  2. 2 hours
  3. 3 hours
  4. 4 hours

3. A runner jogs at 6 mph. Which equation could be used to find the time t (in hours) it takes the runner to go 9 miles?

  1. 6t = 9
  2. t = 6 · 9
  3. 6 + t = 9
  4. t/6 = 9
Show STAAR-style answers and explanations

1. Each travels for 2 hours: bus → 50(2) = 100, car → 60(2) = 120; apart → 100 + 120 = 220 miles → D.

2. Let t = hours. Distance ahead is (70 − 55)t = 15t. Set 15t = 30 → t = 2 → B.

3. d = rt → 9 = 6t → 6t = 9 → A.

Many rate problems are really about relative speed: how much faster one object is compared to another.
