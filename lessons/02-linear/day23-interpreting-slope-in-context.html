Day 23 – Interpreting Slope in Context

Goal: Practice skills with Interpreting Slope in Context.

← Back to all lessons
Key Idea (Interpreting Slope):
Slope is more than a number: it tells how fast one quantity changes compared to another, with units like “miles per hour” or “dollars per item”.

Example (General Algebra)

A function is modeled by d = 60t, where d is distance in miles and t is time in hours. What does the slope 60 mean?

Slope m = 60 means the distance increases by 60 miles every 1 hour. The car is traveling at 60 miles per hour.

Your Turn (General Algebra)

A savings account is modeled by B = 25m + 100, where B is the balance in dollars and m is the number of months. What does the slope 25 represent?

Show answer

Each month, the balance increases by $25 (the deposit or growth per month).

STAAR-Style Practice

1. The height of a plant in centimeters is modeled by h = 3t + 10, where t is the number of weeks. What does the slope 3 represent?

  1. The plant is 3 cm tall at the start.
  2. The plant grows 3 cm each week.
  3. The plant will be 3 cm tall after 10 weeks.
  4. The plant grows 10 cm each week.

2. A function that represents the amount of money in a jar is M = 4n, where n is the number of objects sold. What is the best interpretation of the slope?

  1. There is $4 in the jar at the start.
  2. The jar holds $4 total.
  3. Each object costs $4.
  4. They sell 4 objects every day.

3. A phone battery percentage P changes according to P = 100 − 5t, where t is hours since the battery was full. What does the slope −5 mean?

  1. The battery charges 5% each hour.
  2. The battery loses 5% each hour.
  3. The battery starts at 5%.
  4. The battery lasts for 5 hours.
Show STAAR-style answers and explanations

1. h = 3t + 10 → 3 is “cm per week” → plant grows 3 cm each week → B.

2. M = 4n → 4 is “dollars per object” → each object sold adds $4 → C.

3. The coefficient −5 tells how P changes with t: battery goes down 5 percentage points per hour → B.

Always include units when explaining slope in context: “per hour”, “per day”, “per item”, etc.
