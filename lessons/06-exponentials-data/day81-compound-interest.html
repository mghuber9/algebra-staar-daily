Day 81 – Compound Interest

Goal: Practice skills with Compound Interest.

Key Idea (Compound Interest):
For money that compounds once per year, use
A = P(1 + r)t, where:
• P = starting amount (principal)
• r = annual interest rate (decimal)
• t = number of years
• A = amount after t years

Example (General Algebra)

You deposit $1,000 in an account that pays 5% interest compounded yearly. How much will you have after 4 years?

Step 1 – Identify P, r, t:

  • P = 1000
  • r = 5% = 0.05
  • t = 4

Step 2 – Use the formula:

  • A = 1000(1 + 0.05)⁴ = 1000(1.05)⁴
  • (1.05)⁴ ≈ 1.2155
  • A ≈ 1000(1.2155) ≈ 1,215.50

After 4 years, the account has about $1,215.50.

Your Turn (General Algebra)

You invest $500 at 3% interest compounded yearly. Write a function A(t) for the amount after t years.

Show answer

P = 500, r = 0.03.
A(t) = 500(1.03)t.

STAAR-Style Practice

1. Which function represents $2,000 invested at 4% interest compounded yearly?

  1. A = 2000(0.04)t
  2. A = 2000(1.4)t
  3. A = 2000(1.04)t
  4. A = 2000(0.96)t

2. A savings account balance is modeled by A(t) = 750(1.02)t. What does 1.02 represent?

  1. The annual interest rate of 2%
  2. The starting balance
  3. The interest amount added each year
  4. The total money after 1 year

3. If an account has A(t) = 300(1.06)t, about how much will be in the account after 2 years?

  1. $300
  2. $318
  3. $337
  4. $360
Show STAAR-style answers and explanations

1. Interest 4% → factor = 1.04 → A = 2000(1.04)t → C.

2. 1.02 is the growth factor (100% + 2%) each year → interest rate is 2% → A.

3. A(2) = 300(1.06)² ≈ 300(1.1236) ≈ 337 → C.

For Algebra I, “compound interest” and “exponential growth” use the same formula: starting amount × (1 + r)t.
