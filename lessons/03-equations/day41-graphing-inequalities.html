Key Idea (Graphing Inequalities):
To graph a linear inequality, first graph the related line, then shade the side of the line that makes the inequality true. Use a solid line for ≤ or ≥ and a dashed line for < or >.

Example (General Algebra)

Graph the inequality: y > 2x − 1

Step 1 – Graph the boundary line: Graph y = 2x − 1.

  • Slope m = 2, y-intercept b = −1 → start at (0, −1) and go up 2, right 1.
  • The inequality is > (not ≥), so draw a dashed line.

Step 2 – Choose a test point (usually (0,0) if it’s not on the line):

  • Test (0, 0): Is 0 > 2(0) − 1 ? → 0 > −1 ✔

Step 3 – Shade: Since (0, 0) makes the inequality true, shade the side of the line that contains (0, 0).

Your Turn (General Algebra)

Without drawing, decide if the point (2, 5) is a solution to y ≥ x + 1.

Show answer

Plug in x = 2, y = 5: 5 ≥ 2 + 1 → 5 ≥ 3 ✔.
So (2, 5) is a solution to the inequality.

STAAR-Style Practice

1. Which inequality has a graph with a solid line and shading below the line?

  1. y > −3x + 2
  2. y ≥ −3x + 2
  3. y < −3x + 2
  4. y ≤ −3x + 2

2. The graph of an inequality shows a dashed line for y = x − 4 with shading above the line. Which inequality matches this graph?

  1. y ≥ x − 4
  2. y > x − 4
  3. y ≤ x − 4
  4. y < x − 4

3. A company needs at least $500 in sales. If x is the dollar amount of sales, which graph represents this situation?

  1. A solid dot at 500, arrow to the right on a number line.
  2. A solid dot at 500, arrow to the left on a number line.
  3. An open dot at 500, arrow to the right on a number line.
  4. An open dot at 500, arrow to the left on a number line.
Show STAAR-style answers and explanations

1. Solid line means ≤ or ≥. “Below” matches y ≤ −3x + 2 → D.

2. Dashed line means < or >. Shading above → y > x − 4 → B.

3. “At least 500” means x ≥ 500 → solid dot at 500 and arrow to the right → A.

Remember: solid line/closed dot means the boundary value is included (≤ or ≥). Dashed line/open dot means it is not (< or >).
